Here's the full decoded picture:

**On the 1:1 question specifically** — the answer is an unconditional yes, and it's not a design choice that can be reversed without destroying monetary trust. The e₹ is a direct liability of the RBI, identical in legal standing to a ₹500 note. What *can* change is the *usability* of specific e₹ units — if a subsidy rupee is programmed to only buy fertilizers, that unit has a constrained universe even though it's still worth ₹1. This is a meaningful distinction: programmability doesn't break the peg, it constrains the *where and how* of spending.

**The three things that actually matter long-term:**

The first is **programmable money as a governance tool**. India's DBT subsidy leakage problem (~₹1 lakh crore annually) could be structurally solved by purpose-bound CBDC. This is a bigger policy win than any payment efficiency gain.

The second is **wholesale CBDC + asset tokenization**. This is flying under the radar but the RBI's Unified Markets Interface and Certificate of Deposit tokenization pilots are building the infrastructure for a fully digital capital market in India. This is where the institutional money will flow — fractional ownership of real assets settled in e₹ is a natural next step (relevant to your real estate work).

The third is **the surveillance architecture**. CBDC is not UPI — it generates RBI-visible, fully auditable transaction data natively. Combined with NATGRID, this is the most consequential and least discussed implication. Unlike cash (opaque) or UPI (visible to banks, not always to the state), e₹ gives the sovereign direct sight into every rupee's movement. For KYC-regulated businesses you're tracking, this changes the compliance landscape entirely.
