## Risk #2: The Wisetribes pitch is a beautiful story riding on three operational risks you haven't priced

The brief reads well. The thesis is directionally credible. The longitudinal-data moat argument is genuinely strong if it works. But there are three things the deck waves past:

**(a) "WiseBot humanoid AI robot in classrooms."** This is the most consequential claim in your whole pitch. Is it actually a humanoid form factor, or is it a screen + voice + LLM dressed in marketing language? If it's the former, you have a hardware company hidden inside an edtech company, and Indian edtech-hardware is a graveyard — Lechal, Boltt, several robotics-in-schools attempts. Hardware burns cash on inventory, breaks, requires service infrastructure across 7 cities, and has no margin protection. If it's the latter — you're fine operationally but the marketing claim is overstated and that's a credibility liability with sophisticated investors.

Which one is it? Be honest with yourself. The answer determines whether you need ₹5 cr or ₹50 cr to scale.

**(b) "100+ schools live" needs a precise definition.** Paying contracts? Free pilots? LOIs? MOUs with no payment? The difference between these is the difference between a real business and a vanity metric. What is the ACV per school, what is the collection rate, what is the renewal rate from year 1 to year 2?

**(c) The "2032 harvest" — license memory to other AI products.** This is your terminal moat claim. The honest read: OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, and whatever Indian sovereign-AI players emerge by 2032 will overwhelmingly *not* license third-party memory layers. They will build their own. The realistic version of this moat is "the family pays you for the lifetime companion," not "every other AI in the world plugs into your memory API." The deck overclaims. Trim it before serious investors see it.

**(d) DPDP Act 2023 child data compliance.** You are building a cognitive profile of minors. Under DPDP, this is the highest-compliance-burden category — verifiable parental consent, data minimisation, no tracking/targeted advertising, strict data fiduciary obligations, breach reporting. Your "no advertising ever" line is the right answer, but execution requires a compliance officer and audit infrastructure that you haven't budgeted yet (as far as I can see). Get this priced.

## Risk #3: Wise Creator is a thesis, not a business — and the thesis is fragile

I like the structural intuition. India *is* moving toward more creator regulation — IT Rules 2021/2023, DPDP, PIB fact-check, Broadcasting Services Bill pending. The trajectory is real.

But "Digital Governance Infrastructure" as a business has three structural problems:

1. **Regulatory-arbitrage businesses are the most fragile category of business that exists.** Your moat is a policy decision made by people you don't control. One ministerial change, one Supreme Court ruling, one election cycle, one shift in government approach, and your entire competitive position evaporates. Look at what happened to Aadhaar-stack startups after the SC private-purpose judgement, or eMudhra's regulatory cycles.

2. **The buyer is the government** (or platforms acting under government mandate). Selling to the Indian government requires a level of access, patience, capital reserves, and political navigation that your current network does not have. Janak and Abhishek D are useful contacts. But MeitY / MIB / PMO-grade relationships are an order of magnitude harder. Be honest about the gap.

3. **The creator community will hate you.** "Compliance infrastructure for creators" is, in the eyes of every creator, an extension of state surveillance. If voluntary, no one adopts it. If government-mandated, you become politically tied to whichever party imposed it — and that tie reverses violently when government changes.

**The right way to hold this thesis for the next 12 months:** treat it as research, not a venture. Build a private memo every month tracking (a) regulatory developments, (b) which compliance gaps exist in current platforms, (c) which adjacent companies are emerging, (d) which officials are signalling what. By the time Wisetribes pilot completes in July, you should have 4–5 monthly memos that let you decide whether to launch or kill it — based on evidence, not intuition.
