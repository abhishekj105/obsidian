## The Honest Answer First: There Is No Clean Successor — Yet

Every previous reserve currency transition took **30–70 years** and required a dominant replacement to already be waiting. Right now, no single currency meets all the criteria simultaneously. What we are likely entering is not a *replacement* but a **fragmentation** — which is historically more disruptive than a clean handoff.

## The 5 Contenders — Analysed Seriously

### 1. 🇨🇳 The Chinese Yuan (RMB) — The Obvious Candidate That Probably Won't Win

**The bull case:**
- China is the world's largest trading nation by volume
- The petro-yuan is growing — Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iran, Brazil are settling more oil trades in RMB
- CIPS (China's SWIFT alternative) is expanding
- Belt and Road has created RMB-denominated debt obligations across 140+ countries

**The fatal structural problems:**
- **Capital controls.** A reserve currency must be freely convertible. China refuses to open its capital account fully because doing so would expose the CCP to the kind of speculative attack that destroyed the Thai baht in 1997. You cannot be the world's piggy bank with locked doors.
- **The Triffin Dilemma in reverse.** To supply the world with RMB, China must run persistent trade *deficits* — exporting currency. China's entire economic model is built on *surpluses*. These two things are fundamentally incompatible.
- **Trust deficit.** Reserve currency status requires foreigners to hold your currency in vast quantities for decades. That requires rule of law, independent courts, and property rights protection. China offers none of these reliably.
- **Prof Jiang's own framework** suggests China prefers economic *leverage* over currency *dominance* — Belt and Road is about debt dependency, not RMB internationalisation. China wants to be the lender, not the reserve bank.

**Verdict:** Significant regional role, particularly in Asia and Africa. Global reserve currency — unlikely in the next 20 years without fundamental political restructuring.

---

### 2. 🇪🇺 The Euro — The Perpetual Almost

**The bull case:**
- Largest single market bloc globally
- Deep, liquid bond markets
- Already second-largest reserve currency (~20% of global reserves vs dollar's ~58%)
- European rearmament post-Ukraine is building genuine strategic autonomy

**The fatal structural problems:**
- **No fiscal union.** The euro's fundamental design flaw — one monetary policy, 20 fiscal policies — was exposed in 2010–2012 and has never been fixed. German fiscal conservatism and Southern European spending needs are permanently in tension.
- **No eurobond market of sufficient depth.** The US Treasury market is the world's deepest, most liquid safe asset. Europe has no equivalent single "risk-free" asset.
- **Demographic decline + energy dependency.** The Iran war directly hits Europe hardest — it has no domestic energy base and an ageing workforce.
- **Strategic dependency on the US** for defence still constrains genuine monetary independence.

**Verdict:** Gains share in a fragmenting world, particularly in its neighbourhood. Cannot replace the dollar globally. More likely outcome: Euro becomes dominant in the "Western bloc" while dollar declines to first-among-equals rather than hegemon.

---

### 3. 🇨🇭🇯🇵 Swiss Franc & Japanese Yen — Safe Haven, Not Reserve Currency

These are the **flight-to-safety currencies**, not reserve currency candidates. They rise *during* dollar weakness but cannot absorb the volume of global trade settlement and reserve holding that dollar status requires.

- Switzerland's GDP is ~$800 billion. The world cannot store its reserves in a currency that small without drowning it.
- Japan has the world's largest public debt-to-GDP ratio (~260%). The yen is a safe haven because of Japan's current account surplus and net creditor status — but that model is under stress from demographics and energy costs.

**Verdict:** Strong tactical plays during transition turbulence (as your *war_assets.jsx* dashboard already captures — FXF, FXY as short-term safe havens). Not long-term reserve candidates.

---

### 4. 🪙 Gold — The Non-Currency Reserve Asset

This is the sleeper answer that is actually happening right now. Central banks globally — led by China, Russia, India, Turkey, and Poland — have been buying gold at the fastest rate since 1967. The implicit logic:

- If you don't trust US Treasuries (because the US froze Russia's $300 billion in reserves in 2022), you hold gold
- Gold cannot be sanctioned, frozen, or inflated away
- It requires no trust in any foreign government's rule of law

This is not a gold standard revival — it's **de-dollarisation without a designated heir.** Countries are increasing their gold reserves precisely *because* there is no trusted alternative yet. Gold fills the vacuum.

**Verdict:** The most important non-dollar reserve asset of the transition period. Your gold dashboard thesis — $9,900 by end 2027 — sits directly on top of this structural shift.

---

### 5. 🌐 A Basket / SDR / BRICS Currency — The Multilateral Dream

BRICS nations have discussed a common currency or a commodity-backed basket. The theoretical appeal:
- Backed by a basket of commodities (oil, gold, rare earths) that BRICS collectively dominate
- No single nation controls it — reducing trust requirements
- Directly challenges petrodollar recycling

**The practical problems:**
- Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa have *profoundly* misaligned interests, political systems, and economic models
- India refuses to cede monetary sovereignty to a China-dominated basket
- Russia needs hard currency now, not a decades-long institution-building project
- No enforcement mechanism, no lender of last resort, no deep liquid market

**Verdict:** A long-term aspiration, not a near-term reality. May emerge in 15–25 years if BRICS cohesion survives — which is not guaranteed.
